Down With Robin Hood

 

 

Robin Hood Law has taken more than $3 billion from schools to fund other areas of the state budget.

Learn More
 

The Basics

 

A series of lawsuits in the 1980's and early 1990's pointing out the discrimination of poor school districts lead to the passing of Senate Bill 7, nicknamed the “Robin Hood” law. This law aimed to “recapture” property tax revenue from property-wealthy districts and redistribute it to school districts. When recapture began in 1994, the state collected $127 million from schools. This figure has now grown to over $4 billion for the 2022-23 school year. Out of the 1.2 million students who's schools are victims of recapture, 56% are deemed economically-disadvantaged. Not 100% of this money goes back to schools. It is used in other areas of the state budget.

What can
you do?

Educate yourself on the effects of recapture

• This system was built to provide equal education for all students in Texas, but the rising cost of education is leaving schools paying the state more than they can afford.

Communicate with your legislator

• Let your legislator know that you don't agree with the system. You can mention the growth of recapture, the use of recaptured funds in other areas of the state budget, and transparency in property-tax bills.

Educate other tax-payers in your community

• Now that you know a little more about this system and its effects, help spread awareness within your community by posting to social media.
